Why Monitor Earth Surface?

PURPOSE Why We Monitor

  • Understand regional to global environmental phenomena
  • Environmental degradation & climate change monitoring
  • Identify nature, extent & severity of degradation
  • Feed quantitative data into climate/hydro models

WHAT Surface Properties

  • Terrestrial: Vegetation, Soil, Snow
  • Ocean properties
  • Where is it? → Location/type
  • How much? → Amount / biomass
  • What condition? → Physical property

VEG Vegetation Measures

  • Amount: Biomass, LAI (Leaf Area Index)
  • Structure: LAI, canopy architecture
  • Condition: Pigment content, moisture stress

Spectral Regions of Vegetation

UVVisible (400–700nm)NIR (700–1300nm)SWIR (1300–2500nm)
VISIBLE

Chlorophyll absorbs red & blue light for photosynthesis. Reflects green → plants look green. Low red reflectance = high chlorophyll activity.

NEAR-INFRARED (NIR)

Vegetation reflects strongly (~40-50%). Driven by internal leaf cellular structure (mesophyll). High NIR = healthy/abundant vegetation. Soil also reflects here.

SHORTWAVE INFRARED (SWIR)

Dominated by liquid water absorption. High SWIR reflectance = dry/built-up areas. Used for moisture stress, built-up indices.

What Makes a Good Vegetation Index?

IDEAL VI Criteria (Jackson 1983)

  • Sensitive to vegetative cover
  • Insensitive to soil background
  • Little affected by atmospheric effects
  • Not affected by solar illumination geometry
  • Not affected by sensor viewing conditions

GOALS What VIs Should Do

  • Indicate AMOUNT of vegetation (% cover, LAI, biomass)
  • Distinguish soil vs vegetation
  • Reduce atmospheric effects if possible
  • Reduce topographic effects
  • Better sensitivity than single spectral bands

Soil Reflectance — The Problem

SOIL Soil Behaviour

  • Can be bright in NIR (like vegetation!) — especially dry soil
  • Can have low red reflectance (like vegetation!)
  • Wet soil = much darker than dry soil
  • BUT: NIR–Red difference for soil is much less than for live vegetation
  • Soil line: both wet/dry soil lie on a diagonal in Red–NIR space

PROCESS Ideal Workflow

  • Step 1: Convert DN → Spectral Radiance
  • Step 2: Atmospheric correction → Spectral Reflectance
  • Step 3: Estimate Vegetation Index from reflectance
  • Always use surface reflectance not raw DN for VIs

NDVI Value Reference Table

−1.0 to 0●Water bodies
−0.1 to 0.1●Barren rocks, sand, snow
0.2 to 0.5●Shrubs, grasslands, senescing crops
0.6 to 1.0●Dense vegetation / tropical rainforest
≈ 1 (soil)●SR = 1 for soil; >1 for vegetation (SR index)

Key Limitations to Remember

LIMITS General VI Caveats

  • Background effects: soil reflectance changes index
  • Atmospheric effects: red scattered more than NIR
  • Viewing angle & illumination geometry
  • Statistical relationships — site & species specific
  • Mixed pixels: multiple land covers per pixel
  • Sensor-specific bands: results differ between satellites

NDVI SPECIFIC NDVI Weaknesses

  • Saturates at high biomass — can't distinguish dense forests
  • Atmospheric scatter reduces NDVI values
  • Sensitive to soil background
  • Different soils beneath canopy → different NDVI
  • Correlated to many different things — interpretation ambiguous

Vegetation Indices

Difference Vegetation Index (DVI)

SIMPLEST
DVI = NIR − Red
Range: Unbounded (depends on sensor values)
Pros: Sensitive to vegetation amount; distinguishes soil from vegetation.
Cons: Does NOT handle atmospheric effects or shadows. Not normalized.

Simple Ratio (SR)

FIRST TRUE VI
SR = NIR / Red
SR = 1 for soil | SR > 1 for vegetation | Unbounded upper limit

Measures the slope of the line between the origin of Red–NIR space and the pixel value.

Pros: First true vegetation index; simple.
Cons: Sensor noise is amplified; very large range makes interpretation/scaling difficult.
Example: High biomass (Red=10, NIR=50) → SR = 5  |  Low biomass (Red=20, NIR=30) → SR = 1.5

Normalised Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI)

MOST USED
NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red)
−1 ≤ NDVI ≤ +1

Proposed by Rouse et al., 1973. Normalising constrains values to −1 to +1.

NIR Near-infrared reflectance
Red Red band reflectance
Water: NIR ≈ 0 so NDVI is negative (−1 to 0)  |  Soil: NIR ≈ Red → NDVI ≈ 0  |  Dense veg: NIR ≫ Red → NDVI → +1 (but saturates)
High biomass example: (50−10)/(50+10) = 40/60 ≈ 0.66
Low biomass example: (30−20)/(30+20) = 10/50 = 0.20

Soil Adjusted Vegetation Index (SAVI)

SOIL CORRECTED
SAVI = (NIR − Red) × (1 + L) / (NIR + Red + L)
−1 ≤ SAVI ≤ +1  |  L = 0.5 (standard soil adjustment factor)
L Soil brightness correction factor = 0.5
Why L=0.5? Empirically determined for intermediate vegetation density. L=1 for very sparse vegetation, L=0 → same as NDVI (dense canopy).
Key advantage over NDVI: Accounts for soil background reflectance variation.

Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI)

MODIS / ADVANCED
EVI = G × (NIR − Red) / (NIR + C₁×Red − C₂×Blue + L)
G = 2.5 (gain factor)
C₁ = 6 (aerosol resistance)
C₂ = 7.5 (aerosol resistance)
L ≈ 1 (canopy background)

Proposed by Huete et al., 2002. Uses MODIS NIR, Red, and Blue bands.

Advantages over NDVI:
1. Blue band corrects residual atmospheric contamination
2. L factor minimises soil background effect
3. Sensitive to HIGH biomass areas where NDVI saturates

MERIS Terrestrial Chlorophyll Index (MTCI)

CHLOROPHYLL
MTCI = (R_Band10 − R_Band9) / (R_Band9 − R_Band8)
0 to ~8 (unbounded in theory)
Band 8 ~665 nm (Red)
Band 9 ~709 nm (Red edge)
Band 10 ~754 nm (NIR)

Proposed by Dash & Curran, 2004. Uses the "red edge" — the steep slope in vegetation reflectance between 680–780nm that shifts with chlorophyll content.

With more chlorophyll: Band10−Band9 INCREASES; Band9−Band8 DECREASES → MTCI increases.
ESA standard Level-2 product. Limited atmospheric sensitivity.

Built-Up & Water Indices

Normalised Difference Built-up Index (NDBI)

URBAN
NDBI = (SWIR − NIR) / (SWIR + NIR)
−1 ≤ NDBI ≤ +1
Built-up/bare soil: high SWIR, lower NIR → positive NDBI.
Water: negative NDBI. Vegetation: low/negative NDBI (NIR > SWIR for green surfaces).

Built-Up Index (BU)

URBAN MAPPING
BU = NDBI − NDVI
Binary result: high positive values = built-up / barren.
Subtracting NDVI removes vegetation signal. Water has very low BU (both NDBI and NDVI negative/low).

Normalised Difference Water Index (NDWI / MNDWI)

WATER
NDWI = (NIR − SWIR) / (NIR + SWIR) [Gao, 1996]
MNDWI = (Green − SWIR) / (Green + SWIR) [Xu, 2005]
Water NDWI > 0.5 | Vegetation: small values | Built-up: 0–0.2
Water reflects strongly in blue-green visible, absorbs NIR and SWIR completely.
MNDWI (Modified) uses Green instead of NIR — performs better for urban water extraction.

NDVI vs EVI — Decision Guide

Use NDVI when…

  • Working with ANY sensor (Landsat, SPOT, Sentinel-2)
  • Monitoring seasonal changes in temperate vegetation
  • You need a long historical archive for comparison
  • Vegetation density is LOW to MODERATE
  • Quick, general screening of land cover

Use EVI when…

  • Working with MODIS data specifically
  • Studying tropical/dense boreal forests
  • Atmosphere is hazy or aerosol-rich (fires, dust)
  • You need LAI or canopy structural information
  • Soil background is variable and problematic

Use SAVI when…

  • Vegetation cover is SPARSE (<30% canopy)
  • Soil is highly reflective (arid/bright soils)
  • NDVI is overestimating vegetation in bare areas
  • Early crop season mapping on agricultural fields

Use MTCI when…

  • Need chlorophyll (not just biomass) information
  • Assessing plant health, nutrition, or stress
  • Using MERIS or Sentinel-3 OLCI data
  • Precision agriculture or detailed crop physiology

Agricultural & Crop Monitoring

Q1 A farmer in a semi-arid region of India wants to monitor the health of his wheat crop from satellite imagery during the growing season. The soil in the region is sandy and highly reflective. Which vegetation index would you recommend and why? What precaution must be taken when extracting the index? ▾

Recommended index: SAVI (Soil Adjusted Vegetation Index)

NDVI would be problematic here because the highly reflective sandy soil can increase the NIR reflectance signal, artificially inflating NDVI values and making sparse early-season crops appear denser than they are. SAVI corrects for this using the soil adjustment factor L (typically 0.5), which accounts for the soil brightness signal beneath the canopy.

As the season progresses and canopy cover increases, EVI could also be considered if MODIS data is available, as it further minimises soil and atmospheric effects.

Precautions: (1) Atmospheric correction must be applied first — convert DN → radiance → surface reflectance before computing any index. (2) The L factor should ideally be calibrated for the specific soil type. (3) Results may be sensor-specific — Landsat SAVI and MODIS SAVI may yield slightly different values.

Key terms to use: soil background effect, soil line, L factor, surface reflectance, atmospheric correction, mixed pixels
Q2 A government agency wants to detect nitrogen deficiency in a paddy field in Andhra Pradesh using remote sensing before the crop shows visible symptoms. Which spectral index is most appropriate and what physical principle underpins it? ▾

Recommended index: MTCI (MERIS Terrestrial Chlorophyll Index)

Nitrogen deficiency in plants directly reduces chlorophyll production — chlorophyll requires nitrogen as a key building block. Therefore, detecting early nitrogen stress means measuring chlorophyll content in the canopy, not just biomass amount.

Physical principle: Chlorophyll absorbs red light (~665 nm, Band 8) strongly. As chlorophyll content increases, the "red edge" — the steep rise in vegetation reflectance from ~680 to 780 nm — shifts toward longer wavelengths. MTCI captures this by computing the ratio of reflectance differences in Bands 9–10 (near red edge) to Bands 8–9 (red edge lower slope). A lower MTCI indicates lower chlorophyll, which correlates with nitrogen stress.

NDVI would not be sensitive enough here because it captures biomass/greenness broadly and saturates, not specifically tracking chlorophyll biochemistry.

Key terms: chlorophyll content, red edge, nitrogen deficiency, MERIS bands, biochemical indicator, plant stress detection
Q3 Two satellite images of the same agricultural field are taken — one in April (growing season) and one in October (post-harvest). An analyst finds that the NDVI in April is 0.65 but in October is only 0.08. What do these values tell us, and what limitation of NDVI should the analyst be aware of when comparing results across time? ▾

Interpretation: NDVI of 0.65 in April indicates dense, actively growing and photosynthetically active vegetation (crops at peak growth). NDVI of 0.08 in October is close to zero, consistent with bare ground or harvested stubble with minimal live vegetation.

This demonstrates NDVI's usefulness for phenological monitoring — tracking the seasonal growth cycle and assessing the length of the growing season.

Limitations to note:

1. Atmospheric effects: If the atmosphere was hazier in one acquisition, red light is scattered more than NIR, which reduces NDVI values even if nothing changed on the ground. Atmospheric correction is essential for multi-temporal comparison.

2. Soil background: In October, the exposed soil brightness affects the index — different soil moisture conditions between seasons add noise.

3. Sensor inconsistency: If images are from different sensors, band widths differ, making direct NDVI comparison invalid without cross-calibration.

Key terms: phenology, seasonal NDVI profile, atmospheric correction, multi-temporal analysis, DN vs reflectance

Forest & Tropical Vegetation

Q4 A researcher is mapping deforestation in the Amazon rainforest using MODIS data. She finds that NDVI fails to distinguish between areas of intact forest and areas recovering from selective logging, even though ground measurements show a 40% difference in biomass. Explain why this happens and suggest a better approach. ▾

Problem: NDVI saturation at high biomass.

NDVI is based on a ratio of NIR and Red reflectance. At high vegetation densities (like tropical forest), the red band is almost completely absorbed by chlorophyll and cannot be further reduced — it effectively hits a floor. At the same time, NIR increases only marginally with additional leaf layers. The result is that NDVI saturates — it plateaus even when biomass continues to increase, making it impossible to distinguish dense forest from slightly less dense recovering forest.

Better approach: EVI (Enhanced Vegetation Index)

EVI was specifically designed for this situation. It uses MODIS bands (NIR, Red, Blue) and includes a canopy background correction (L factor) and aerosol resistance term (using Blue band). EVI remains sensitive to LAI and canopy differences in high-biomass environments where NDVI has already saturated. It is also less affected by the haze and aerosols common in the Amazon (from fires).

Key terms: NDVI saturation, high biomass, EVI, LAI sensitivity, MODIS, aerosol resistance, tropical forest monitoring

Urban & Land Use

Q5 A city planner wants to track urban expansion in a rapidly growing city using Landsat satellite data from 2000 and 2020. Which index/indices would you use? Describe how you would map the new built-up areas and what confounding factors you must consider. ▾

Recommended approach: NDBI + BU Index

NDBI (Normalised Difference Built-up Index) = (SWIR − NIR)/(SWIR + NIR). Built-up areas and bare soil reflect more SWIR than NIR, giving positive NDBI values. Water gives negative values.

The BU (Built-up Index) = NDBI − NDVI subtracts the vegetation signal, producing a binary-like map where high positive values indicate built-up or barren land and removing false positives from vegetated areas.

Workflow: (1) Compute NDBI for both 2000 and 2020 images. (2) Compute BU for both. (3) Compare — areas that shifted from low/negative BU to high positive BU between dates represent new urban development.

Confounding factors:

1. Dry bare soil also gives high NDBI — new construction areas may be confused with agricultural bare fields or rocky outcrops. Seasonal timing of image acquisition matters.

2. Atmospheric correction must be consistent between 2000 and 2020 images.

3. Different Landsat sensors (TM vs OLI) have different band widths — cross-calibration needed.

Key terms: SWIR reflectance, NDBI, BU index, urban sprawl, impervious surfaces, change detection, seasonal timing

Water Bodies & Flood Mapping

Q6 Following a severe monsoon flood event in a coastal district of Odisha, disaster management officials need to rapidly map the extent of flooded area using satellite imagery. Which index would you use and why? How would NDVI compare in effectiveness? ▾

Recommended index: MNDWI (Modified NDWI)

Water has essentially zero reflectance in NIR and SWIR (it absorbs both), but reflects in the visible green spectrum. MNDWI = (Green − SWIR)/(Green + SWIR) exploits this: flooded pixels give high positive MNDWI values (>0.5), while land (vegetation or built-up) gives low or negative values.

MNDWI is preferred over original NDWI for areas with mixed urban and water features because it uses the SWIR band which better separates water from built-up surfaces.

Comparison with NDVI: NDVI would actually give negative values over water (since NIR ≈ 0), which could help identify flooded areas indirectly. However, flooded crops and vegetation also show reduced NDVI, making it hard to distinguish between "permanently inundated" vs "stressed/submerged vegetation." MNDWI directly targets the water signal and is more reliable for flood extent mapping.

Practical note: Pre- and post-flood comparison using MNDWI change maps allows precise delineation of newly inundated areas.

Key terms: MNDWI, water reflectance, NIR absorption, green band, flood inundation, change detection, NDWI vs MNDWI

Multi-Index Reasoning

Q7 An analyst extracts NDVI from a satellite image over a region and gets the following pixel values: Pixel A = 0.65, Pixel B = 0.04, Pixel C = −0.35, Pixel D = 0.15. For each pixel, identify the likely land cover type and explain the physical reasoning behind each NDVI value. ▾

Pixel A (NDVI = 0.65): Dense, healthy vegetation — likely forest or agricultural crop at peak growth. High NIR (from internal mesophyll structure) and very low red (absorbed by chlorophyll) gives high positive ratio.

Pixel B (NDVI = 0.04): Bare soil or very sparse vegetation. NIR and Red are nearly equal, so the ratio approaches zero. Could also be urban/built-up area or sand.

Pixel C (NDVI = −0.35): Water body. Water absorbs NIR almost completely, so NIR ≈ 0. Red reflectance is slightly higher (water reflects somewhat in visible blue-green). NIR − Red becomes negative → negative NDVI.

Pixel D (NDVI = 0.15): Shrubland, grassland, or senescing/stressed crops. Some vegetation present but NIR–Red difference is modest. Could also be sparse dryland vegetation or areas recovering after disturbance.

Exam tip: Always link the NDVI value to the physical spectral behaviour — chlorophyll absorption of red, leaf internal structure causing high NIR, water absorbing NIR. Don't just say "high = vegetation."
Q8 A scientist wants to compare vegetation health across two study sites: Site A is a dense tropical forest in the Western Ghats, and Site B is a sparse savanna in Rajasthan. She has MODIS imagery for both. Design a complete remote sensing methodology, choosing appropriate indices for each site and justifying your choices. ▾

Site A — Dense tropical forest (Western Ghats): Use EVI + MTCI

NDVI is inappropriate because it will saturate — both the intact forest and any degraded patches will show similar NDVI values (~0.8+). EVI is designed for high-biomass environments and remains sensitive to canopy differences. Using MODIS EVI products (MOD13A) gives consistent time-series data. MTCI (if MERIS/Sentinel-3 data available) could assess chlorophyll to monitor forest health and photosynthetic capacity.

Site B — Sparse savanna (Rajasthan): Use SAVI + NDVI time series

Sparse vegetation means lots of exposed bright soil between plants. NDVI would overestimate vegetation due to high soil reflectance. SAVI with L=0.5 corrects for this. Seasonal NDVI time series can also show the pulse of vegetation greenup tied to monsoon rainfall.

Common methodology steps for both:

1. Atmospheric correction: convert DN → surface reflectance. 2. Select cloud-free composites. 3. Compute appropriate index. 4. Validate against ground measurements where possible. 5. Note sensor-specific band definitions.

Key concept: No single index is best for all situations — the choice depends on vegetation density, soil type, available sensor, and what property you are measuring (amount vs condition).
